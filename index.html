âœ“ Growth is evidenced by increasing consistency at higher levels over time

COMPETENCY 1: DATA STORYTELLING

Transforming complex data insights into compelling narratives that drive informed decision-making across diverse stakeholder groups

Fundamental
Building Narrative Foundations
Students construct basic data narratives with clear structure, connect findings to business context, and begin adapting communication for different audiences. They organize information logically and use visualizations to support key points, though translation from technical to business language is still developing.

Learning Outcomes - Students Can:

  • Organize findings into a coherent narrative structure (setup, analysis, conclusion)
  • Connect data observations to relevant business implications and context
  • Create appropriate visualizations that support primary messages
  • Present recommendations with basic justification from data evidence
  • Identify target audience needs and adjust terminology accordingly
  • Distinguish between descriptive reporting and insight generation
Intermediate
Strategic Communication
Students craft compelling, audience-tailored narratives that seamlessly integrate data, visualization, and business strategy. They independently translate complex technical findings into actionable insights, design sophisticated visualizations, and construct well-reasoned recommendations that address stakeholder priorities and concerns.

Learning Outcomes - Students Can:

  • Develop complete story arcs that balance technical rigor with accessibility
  • Design visualizations that reveal patterns and support decision-making
  • Craft actionable recommendations tied to specific business outcomes
  • Adapt communication style, depth, and framing to stakeholder expertise
  • Anticipate and address potential questions or concerns in the narrative
  • Synthesize multiple data sources into a unified, coherent story
  • Use persuasive techniques while maintaining analytical integrity
Advanced
Transformative Influence
Students deliver sophisticated, persuasive narratives that drive strategic decision-making in complex, ambiguous situations. They innovate in visualization design to reveal non-obvious insights, craft multi-layered arguments with implementation roadmaps, and influence organizational direction through exceptional data storytelling that bridges technical excellence and business leadership.

Learning Outcomes - Students Can:

  • Create strategic narratives that preemptively address objections and alternatives
  • Design innovative, publication-quality visualizations that communicate complex relationships
  • Develop comprehensive recommendations with phased implementation plans and success metrics
  • Influence cross-functional stakeholders and facilitate consensus building
  • Navigate politically sensitive or controversial data findings diplomatically
  • Establish narrative frameworks that others can adopt for organizational storytelling
  • Mentor others in effective data communication and presentation skills

ðŸ’¡ Example: Intermediate Data Storytelling in Practice

A student analyzes customer churn data for a retail company. They create a presentation that opens with the business context (quarterly churn has increased to 22%, costing $2M in lost revenue), uses a dashboard visualization showing churn rates by customer segment and purchase frequency, and presents three specific retention strategies with projected ROI. When the VP of Marketing asks about seasonal patterns, the student adjusts their explanation from technical language about time-series decomposition to a business-friendly discussion of "holiday shopping behavior trends." The recommendations include specific implementation steps and success metrics.

This demonstrates: Complete narrative arc, audience adaptation, business-connected recommendations, effective visualization design, and ability to respond to stakeholder questions (Intermediate behaviors #1, #2, #3, #4, #5).

COMPETENCY 2: AI-AUGMENTED ANALYTICS

Leveraging artificial intelligence tools strategically and responsibly to enhance analytical capabilities while maintaining critical evaluation and domain expertise

Fundamental
Guided AI Application
Students use AI tools for standard analytical tasks with developing proficiency in prompt engineering and output evaluation. They understand basic AI capabilities and limitations, compare AI-generated results with traditional methods, and begin to validate outputs using domain knowledge. Critical assessment skills are emerging but require guidance.

Learning Outcomes - Students Can:

  • Construct clear, specific prompts that yield relevant and useful outputs
  • Identify appropriate use cases for AI tools versus traditional analytical methods
  • Detect obvious errors, inconsistencies, or biases in AI-generated content
  • Explain basic AI capabilities, limitations, and ethical considerations
  • Validate AI outputs against known benchmarks or alternative approaches
  • Document AI tool usage and decision-making process in analysis workflows
Intermediate
Strategic AI Integration
Students strategically integrate AI tools into comprehensive analytics workflows, demonstrating sophisticated prompt engineering and systematic validation practices. They independently select optimal tools based on task requirements, critically evaluate outputs using domain expertise, and articulate clear rationale for when AI adds value versus when traditional methods are preferable.

Learning Outcomes - Students Can:

  • Engineer sophisticated prompt sequences for complex, multi-step analytical problems
  • Compare multiple AI tools and select based on task-specific strengths and constraints
  • Develop systematic validation frameworks that combine AI and human expertise
  • Refine and iterate AI outputs through strategic prompting and domain knowledge
  • Identify subtle errors, hallucinations, or quality issues in AI-generated content
  • Balance efficiency gains from AI with maintaining analytical rigor and accuracy
  • Explain AI decision-making to stakeholders in accessible, transparent ways
Advanced
AI Innovation Leadership
Students demonstrate advanced AI literacy across multiple tools and contexts, creating innovative applications that push boundaries of AI-augmented analytics. They develop organizational frameworks for AI adoption, quality assurance, and governance. They lead others in responsible AI integration, establish best practices, and critically evaluate emerging AI capabilities for analytics applications.

Learning Outcomes - Students Can:

  • Design novel AI applications that address complex, previously unsolved analytical challenges
  • Create organizational frameworks for AI tool evaluation, adoption, and quality control
  • Develop training materials and mentor others in effective AI integration
  • Evaluate emerging AI technologies and assess their potential for analytics workflows
  • Navigate ethical dilemmas and edge cases in AI application with principled reasoning
  • Optimize AI-human collaboration workflows for maximum analytical value
  • Contribute to broader discussions on responsible AI use in business analytics

ðŸ’¡ Example: Intermediate AI-Augmented Analytics in Practice

A student uses Claude to help analyze employee sentiment from open-ended survey responses. Rather than accepting the AI's initial summary, they design a multi-step prompt sequence: first extracting themes, then validating those themes against HR literature, then quantifying sentiment by department. They notice the AI misclassifies sarcastic comments as positive, so they refine their prompt to flag ambiguous responses for manual review. They compare AI-identified themes with their own close reading of 50 responses, finding 85% agreement. In their final report, they transparently document their AI workflow, explain why they used AI for initial theme extraction but validated manually, and note the limitations of sentiment analysis on culturally diverse workforce comments.

This demonstrates: Sophisticated prompt engineering, systematic validation approach, identification of AI errors, appropriate tool selection, and transparent documentation (Intermediate behaviors #1, #3, #4, #5, #7).

COMPETENCY 3: ANALYTICAL REASONING

Systematically evaluating data quality, analytical methods, and business problems through rigorous questioning, evidence-based reasoning, and consideration of multiple perspectives

Fundamental
Questioning Foundations
Students move beyond descriptive analysis to evaluate data quality, identify limitations, and consider alternative explanations. They ask probing questions about data sources and collection methods, recognize obvious gaps or inconsistencies, and demonstrate emerging awareness of analytical assumptions. Critical thinking is developing but may require prompting in complex situations.

Learning Outcomes - Students Can:

  • Question data sources, collection methods, and potential quality issues
  • Identify obvious limitations, gaps, or missing information in datasets
  • Distinguish between correlation and causation in analytical findings
  • Consider alternative interpretations when evaluating results
  • Recognize common cognitive biases that affect analytical judgment
  • Articulate assumptions underlying analytical approaches and conclusions
Intermediate
Systematic Evaluation
Students systematically evaluate data quality, analytical rigor, and business logic across complex scenarios. They independently assess data completeness and reliability, evaluate appropriateness of analytical methods, and synthesize insights from multiple perspectives. They acknowledge uncertainty, identify confounding factors, and challenge assumptions throughout the analytical process.

Learning Outcomes - Students Can:

  • Critically assess data sources for reliability, bias, and representativeness
  • Map data processes to identify where information degrades or becomes incomplete
  • Evaluate analytical methods for appropriateness given data characteristics and business context
  • Identify confounding variables and potential sources of spurious relationships
  • Consider multiple competing explanations and weigh evidence systematically
  • Recognize when additional data or alternative approaches are needed
  • Challenge conventional wisdom when data suggests alternative conclusions
Advanced
Strategic Critical Thinking
Students demonstrate sophisticated analytical reasoning in ambiguous, high-stakes situations. They create systematic frameworks for evaluating data, processes, and business problems. They identify non-obvious biases and gaps, synthesize insights across disparate analytical perspectives, and proactively question assumptions that others accept. They lead others in developing critical thinking capabilities.

Learning Outcomes - Students Can:

  • Develop comprehensive evaluation frameworks for complex analytical problems
  • Identify subtle biases, measurement issues, and data quality problems others miss
  • Synthesize conflicting evidence from multiple sources into coherent conclusions
  • Navigate analytical ambiguity and make principled decisions with incomplete information
  • Anticipate how analytical choices propagate uncertainty through decision-making
  • Challenge organizational assumptions and advocate for evidence-based approaches
  • Mentor others in developing rigorous, questioning analytical mindsets

ðŸ’¡ Example: Intermediate Analytical Reasoning in Practice

A student analyzes sales data showing a 30% increase in online orders after a website redesign. Rather than concluding the redesign caused the increase, they investigate further: checking whether a concurrent marketing campaign might confound results, examining whether the increase was consistent across product categories (it wasn'tâ€”only electronics increased), and reviewing whether seasonal patterns could explain the change (the increase started before typical holiday shopping). They discover the data excludes returned items, which spiked 40% for electronics during this period. Their report concludes: "While online orders increased, the redesign's impact is unclear due to confounding factors. The electronics-specific increase and high return rate suggest potential usability issues worth investigating before declaring success."

This demonstrates: Questioning initial conclusions, identifying confounding variables, recognizing data limitations, evaluating multiple explanations, and acknowledging uncertainty (Intermediate behaviors #2, #4, #5, #6).

COMPETENCY 4: COLLABORATIVE ANALYTICS

Contributing effectively to cross-functional team success through active collaboration, knowledge sharing, and integration of diverse perspectives in analytics projects

Fundamental
Active Participation
Students reliably complete assigned tasks, communicate progress effectively, and actively participate in team discussions. They respond to requests from team members, share ideas in meetings, and demonstrate willingness to collaborate. They are developing coordination skills and beginning to see beyond individual contributions to team goals, though may still require direction for integration activities.

Learning Outcomes - Students Can:

  • Complete individual assignments on time with quality that supports team progress
  • Communicate progress, challenges, and needs clearly to team members
  • Actively participate in team meetings and contribute ideas to discussions
  • Respond constructively to feedback and incorporate input from others
  • Assist team members when asked and share relevant knowledge or resources
  • Adapt individual work based on team priorities and changing requirements
Intermediate
Proactive Collaboration
Students proactively drive team success through knowledge sharing, facilitation of productive discussions, and integration of diverse contributions. They leverage different perspectives to strengthen outcomes, adapt flexibly to various team roles, and help coordinate complex deliverables. They demonstrate consistent reliability and actively support team cohesion and effectiveness.

Learning Outcomes - Students Can:

  • Proactively identify opportunities to support team members and share expertise
  • Facilitate productive team discussions that surface diverse viewpoints
  • Integrate individual contributions into cohesive, high-quality deliverables
  • Navigate different team roles (leader, contributor, supporter) as situations require
  • Provide constructive feedback that helps team members improve their work
  • Identify and address coordination challenges before they impact team progress
  • Leverage diverse team member strengths to optimize project outcomes
Advanced
Team Leadership
Students demonstrate leadership in optimizing team performance and navigating complex interpersonal and project challenges. They strategically identify and leverage individual strengths, facilitate conflict resolution, and coordinate multi-faceted projects with competing priorities. They build team culture that encourages innovation, accountability, and continuous improvement while developing collaborative capabilities in others.

Learning Outcomes - Students Can:

  • Strategically match team member strengths to project needs and development opportunities
  • Facilitate resolution of conflicts and obstacles that impede team progress
  • Coordinate complex projects with multiple workstreams and dependencies
  • Build team culture that balances accountability with psychological safety
  • Mentor team members in developing collaboration and project management skills
  • Navigate organizational politics and stakeholder dynamics to support team success
  • Establish processes and norms that enhance team effectiveness and sustainability

In a team project analyzing hospital readmission rates, a student notices their teammate struggling with SQL queries. Rather than just completing their own assigned analysis, they schedule a 30-minute session to walk through query logic, shares a template they created, and suggests documentation resources. During team meetings, they actively solicit input from quieter members ("Dev, you worked in healthcareâ€”what's your take on these patterns?"). When the team's visualizations and written analysis feel disjointed, they volunteer to create a shared outline that integrates everyone's work, then circulate it for feedback before finalizing. When a team member misses a deadline due to a family emergency, they proactively redistribute tasks and update the project timeline, then check in privately to offer support.

This demonstrates: Proactive knowledge sharing, facilitation of inclusive discussions, integration of deliverables, flexible role adaptation, and supportive team culture (Intermediate behaviors #1, #2, #3, #4, #6).

IMPLEMENTATION GUIDANCE

ðŸŽ¯ Core Pedagogical Principles

  • Progressive Mastery: Competencies build cumulativelyâ€”each level integrates and extends prior learning
  • Growth Mindset: All students can reach advanced levels with effort, practice, and effective feedback
  • Authentic Assessment: Evaluate competencies through realistic, applied scenarios that mirror professional practice
  • Transparency: Students learn best when expectations are clear and criteria are explicit

For Faculty: Designing Learning Experiences

Assignment Mapping

  • Align to progression: Map assignments to expected competency levels based on program stage and course objectives
  • Scaffold deliberately: Design assignment sequences that build skills progressively within and across courses
  • Provide rubrics in advance: Share competency levels and criteria before assignments so students understand targets
  • Use consistent language: Reference rubric terminology in instructions, feedback, and grading to reinforce expectations

Effective Feedback Strategies

  • Be specific and actionable: Reference particular observable behaviors and suggest concrete next steps. "Your visualization effectively supported key points (Intermediate behavior #2). To strengthen further, practice anticipating audience questions in your narrative structure (Intermediate behavior #5)."
  • Describe developmental patterns: "You're consistently demonstrating Fundamental capabilities and showing emerging Intermediate skills in X and Y. Focus next on..."
  • Acknowledge growth trajectory: "Compared to your first assignment, you're now regularly demonstrating Intermediate behavior #3. Keep building on this momentum."
  • Balance affirmation with challenge: Recognize current capabilities while identifying the "emerging edge" where students should focus development efforts
  • Connect to future expectations: "This demonstrates solid Fundamental performance. In the next assignment, try incorporating Intermediate behaviors #2 and #4 to continue your progression."
  • Use growth-oriented language: "Not yet" instead of "can't," "developing" instead of "weak," "emerging" instead of "lacking"

For Assessment: Measuring Competency Development

Assessing Along a Continuum

  • Describe patterns, not categories: Instead of "This is a Fundamental student," say "Student consistently demonstrates Fundamental capabilities with emerging Intermediate proficiency in X and Y"
  • Use threshold + trajectory language: "Has established Fundamental threshold and is progressing toward Intermediate in 3 of 4 competencies"
  • Acknowledge context-dependence: "Shows Intermediate performance on familiar tasks; Fundamental on novel, complex problemsâ€”consistent with expected development"
  • Focus on consistency over time: Look for patterns across multiple assignments, not single performances

Assessment Best Practices

  • Select relevant criteria: Not every assignment assesses all behaviorsâ€”choose the most relevant 3-5 for each task
  • Use multiple measures: Assess competencies across several assignments to identify patterns and growth trajectories
  • Distinguish formative from summative: Use rubrics for ongoing developmental feedback and for final threshold evaluation appropriately
  • Track longitudinally: Monitor competency development across course sequences to ensure progression toward program goals
  • Allow for uneven development: Students may advance at different rates in different competenciesâ€”this is expected and reflects individual strengths and priorities

Evidence-Based Evaluation

  • Portfolio approach: Collect evidence of competency across multiple artifacts, contexts, and time points
  • Look for multiple indicators: Identify 3-4 observable behaviors from target level demonstrated across different assignments
  • Inter-rater reliability: When multiple instructors assess competencies, calibrate interpretations through norming sessions
  • Student self-assessment: Include reflection components where students evaluate their own competency development and set growth goals
  • Continuous improvement: Use assessment data to refine curriculum, pedagogy, and support structures

Reporting Student Progress

"Sarah consistently demonstrates Intermediate competency in Data Storytelling and Analytical Reasoning. She shows emerging Intermediate capabilities in AI-Augmented Analytics, particularly in prompt engineering and output validation. Her Collaborative Analytics skills remain at the Fundamental level but are developing, with recent evidence of more proactive knowledge sharing."

Assignment Planning Framework

When designing assignments and mapping them to competencies, consider these guiding questions:

Alignment Questions

  • Level targeting: Which competency level (Fundamental/Intermediate/Advanced) should students demonstrate at this program stage?
  • Observable behaviors: Which 3-5 specific behaviors from the rubric can this assignment authentically assess?
  • Scaffolding: How does this assignment build on prior work and prepare students for subsequent challenges?
  • Integration: Are students demonstrating multiple competencies together, as they would in professional practice?

Support Mechanisms

  • What feedback will students receive? Plan formative checkpoints, peer review, or draft reviews to support development
  • What resources are available? Identify exemplars, templates, tutorials, or office hours that help students succeed
  • How will students reflect? Include metacognitive components where students assess their own competency growth
  • What happens when students struggle? Define intervention strategies and support pathways for students below expectations

Practical Implementation: Making This Work

Using This Framework Realistically

This framework is comprehensive by design, but practical implementation requires selectivity and strategy:

  • Not every assignment assesses all 4 competenciesâ€”select 1-2 per assignment that align with learning objectives
  • Not every behavior needs assessmentâ€”focus on 3-4 priority behaviors per assignment rather than all 6-7
  • Use formative assessment liberallyâ€”provide developmental comments more often than formal rubric scoring
  • Leverage technologyâ€”use rubric features in Canvas/Blackboard to reduce manual tracking and provide consistency
  • Start small and scaleâ€”pilot with one competency in one course before expanding to full program implementation

Reducing Faculty Burden

  • Create quick-reference guides: One-page summaries of just the behaviors list for each competency
  • Build assignment-specific rubrics: Pull only relevant behaviors for each task rather than using the full framework
  • Share the assessment load: Different faculty can focus on different competencies within courses
  • Use peer assessment strategically: Students can provide formative feedback on selected behaviors, reserving faculty assessment for summative evaluation
  • Batch similar assignments: Assess the same competency across multiple students simultaneously for calibration and efficiency

Building Over Time

  • Year 1: Focus on establishing reliable Fundamental assessment and faculty norming
  • Year 2: Add Intermediate tracking and begin building exemplar library from student work
  • Year 3+: Implement full developmental tracking with portfolio assessment and program-level analytics

Equity and Inclusive Practice

This framework recognizes that students enter the MSBA program with varied educational backgrounds, work experiences, cultural contexts, and learning needs. To implement this framework equitably:

Recognizing Diverse Strengths

  • Communication styles vary culturally: Effective data storytelling may look different across contexts. Focus on clarity, impact, and audience connection rather than conformity to a single presentation style. What counts as "professional communication" in one culture may differ in another.
  • Prior experience shapes starting points: Students from industry bring different strengths than those coming straight from undergraduate programs. Students with technical backgrounds may start stronger in analytical reasoning but need more support in storytelling. Recognize and leverage these diverse entry points.
  • Multiple valid paths to competency: Some students excel in written analysis, others in verbal presentation, others in visual communication. Provide multiple ways to demonstrate competency where possible.

Practical Equity Strategies

  • Provide explicit instruction on norms: Don't assume students know "professional business communication" normsâ€”teach them explicitly with examples. This particularly supports first-generation students and international students.
  • Use diverse examples and scenarios: Ensure case studies, datasets, and scenarios reflect varied industries, geographic contexts, and organizational types.
  • Scaffold collaborative work: Teamwork competencies may be new to students from educational systems emphasizing individual work. Provide explicit frameworks and support for effective collaboration.
  • Be mindful of language barriers: For multilingual students, distinguish between language proficiency and analytical capability. Consider extended time or alternative formats when appropriate.
  • Address technology access: Ensure AI tools and software used in competency development are accessible to all students, not just those with personal resources.

Accessibility and Accommodations

  • Work with disability services: Adapt assessment methods while maintaining competency standards for students with documented disabilities
  • Consider universal design: Build flexibility into assessments (e.g., written report OR recorded presentation) that benefits all students
  • Provide alternative formats: When presentations are required, consider allowing pre-recorded videos for students with anxiety or speech differences
Ongoing Commitment: Equitable implementation requires regular reflection on whose voices, experiences, and ways of demonstrating competency are privileged by our assessments. Faculty should periodically review assessment data disaggregated by student demographics to identify unintended bias patterns and adjust accordingly.

For Students: Understanding Your Development

Navigating the Competency Continuum

  • Expect uneven development: It's normal to be stronger in some competencies than others. Your profile will be unique and should align with your career interests
  • Understand that context matters: You might perform at Intermediate level on familiar tasks but revert to Fundamental approaches with new, complex challenges. This is expected!
  • Focus on trajectory, not just current state: Ask yourself "Am I demonstrating higher-level skills more consistently than last month?" rather than "What level am I?"
  • Recognize that setbacks are part of growth: Struggling with new material doesn't mean you've regressedâ€”it means you're pushing your boundaries

Self-Assessment Strategies

  • Review your work against rubrics: For each assignment, identify which 3-4 behaviors you demonstrated and which you're still developing
  • Look for patterns over time: Are you showing Intermediate behaviors more frequently? In more contexts? With less support?
  • Identify your "emerging edge": What's the next level behavior you're starting to demonstrate? Focus deliberate practice there
  • Map your competency profile: Where are you strongest? Where do you want to grow? How does this align with your career goals?

Setting Effective Growth Goals

  • Be specific: Instead of "Get better at storytelling," try "Consistently design visualizations that reveal patterns (Intermediate behavior #2)"
  • Target adjacent skills: If you're solid at Fundamental, focus on 2-3 Intermediate behaviors, not jumping to Advanced
  • Seek varied practice: Demonstrate emerging skills across different contexts to build consistency
  • Request targeted feedback: Ask instructors/peers to evaluate you on specific behaviors you're working to develop
  • Reflect regularly: Monthly self-assessment helps you notice growth that might feel invisible day-to-day

Getting the Most from Feedback

  • Ask for developmental feedback: "What emerging capabilities do you see?" not just "What level am I?"
  • Identify your next steps: What specific behaviors should you practice to strengthen your trajectory?
  • Compare across assignments: How has your performance evolved over the semester?
  • Celebrate growth: Acknowledge when you demonstrate new capabilities, even if not yet consistent
